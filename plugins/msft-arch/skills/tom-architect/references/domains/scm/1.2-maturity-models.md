The Cost-to-Serve (CTS) and Customer Service Levels (CSL) are optimized. Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) is an integral part of the multi-year logistics strategy.
Multiple channels are defined but remain loosely affiliated, limiting the achievement of maximum synergies. The network plan is shaped by the desired customer value.
Network design is based on balancing Cost-to-Serve (CTS) and Customer Service Level (CSL) objectives. Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) is an integral part of Supply Chain Management (SCM).
A three-to-five-year network plan is aligned with the company plan and linked to an Integrated Business Plan (IBP). The organization utilizes a network of trusted Third-Party Logistics (3PL) providers to fulfill customer needs.
Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) measures and targets are regularly monitored by a strategic agile team for effective management and continuous improvement.
Network design is efficient and effective, with an understanding of customer profitability in relation to the network design and associated Cost-to-Serve (CTS) and Customer Service Level (CSL) factors.
Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) KPIs drive warehouse management operations and material movement within the network.
Multiple channels are defined, but there is no plan to improve service and reduce Transportation and Warehousing (T&W) costs. Customer Service Levels (CSL) and Cost-to-Serve (CTS) objectives are reviewed periodically.
A fragmented Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) process is aligned with annual transportation plans.
The absence of a logistics plan leads to reactive measures to ensure product supply supports revenue targets. The number and types of channels to market are unknown, as is the logistics support required for them.
There is no technology available to support Transportation and Warehousing (T&W) scenario-based modeling and analysis. A Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) system is not in place.
The organization may have access to basic tools within an Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system but primarily relies on Excel or advanced tools provided by a third-party technology provider. A stand-alone Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) system is utilized.
Basic planning tools, along with simulation software (e.g., for optimizing the logistics network), are in place but may not be fully integrated with the Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system and other discrete systems. The Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) system is based on an integrated database system.
The sourcing process prioritizes carriers that offer best-in-class technology for enhanced logistics performance.
Constraint-based supply chain planning and optimization tools are implemented for effective resource management. Advanced planning tools with simulation capabilities, such as Warehouse Management Systems (WMS), provide a proactive view of required design changes to optimize the logistics network.
Advanced technologies, such as drones, are employed for warehouse management. Enhanced analytics capabilities, along with demand sensing technology, are enabled through the Internet of Things (IoT) and other Industry 4.0 assets for improved efficiency and foresight.
Basic spreadsheets are utilized to establish baseline discrete logistics data. Logistics Requirements Planning (LRP) is managed ad-hoc through manual worksheets.
Data may be available but requires substantial effort to collect and organize. Costs and revenues are often incomplete or available only at aggregate levels. Monthly reporting is supplemented by weekly flash reports.
Data is available within supply chain planning tools and Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems but may not be fully reconciled. Financial reporting and forecasting are integrated with operational metrics for a cohesive analysis.
Clear roles and accountability are defined, with direct links to Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) for performance measurement and management.
No data is collected or available to represent supply chain flows, costs, and capacities.
Basic data is stored in spreadsheets and is not aligned with organizational policies. Reporting may include flash reports but is primarily based on monthly or quarterly results.
Automated data collection is facilitated through digital platforms, Internet of Things (IoT), and sensors, enabling the preparation of financial, flow, and volumetric data. Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are automatically measured and reported consistently.
Advanced analytics tools support evidence-based human decision-making, facilitating informed choices. A comprehensive sustainability and Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) impact analysis is conducted for material issues.
ESG data is made available to the entire team, promoting transparency and shared understanding of the organization's ESG performance.

There is a lack of network design capability and responsibility, along with a limited understanding of its impact on cost and service. No clear ownership exists for Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM).
Limited network design capability exists but is not integrated within the central team. Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) is led functionally, with ad-hoc and non-directed network plans in place.
There is a reliance on a few basic Profit and Loss (P&L) codes for identifying specific logistics costs.
There is an awareness of Cost-to-Serve (CTS) and Customer Service Level (CSL) impacts on network design. Network planning and Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) responsibilities are assigned to a central supply chain team member.
Individual sites have a degree of cost and measurement accountability for their performance and contributions.
Network design responsibilities are assigned to senior supply chain staff and implemented by a central team with the support of site teams. A dedicated Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) team manages an extended risk program across the network.
Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) training plans have been developed and implemented within the organization for all stakeholders.
The logistics plan is managed by the core supply chain team, which reports to the Chief Executive Officer (CEO) or Chief Operating Officer (COO). A Fourth-Party Logistics Provider (4PL) may be used, reporting directly to the General Manager (GM) of supply chain.
Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) is incorporated within the overall Enterprise Risk Management framework for holistic risk assessment and mitigation.
Logistics arrangements are determined by individual sites. Transportation and warehousing strategies are influenced by cost drivers rather than customer needs. Network structure is driven by historical growth patterns.
Sustainability is perceived as a cost burden rather than a strategic priority.
The logistics plan may be outsourced to a Third-Party Logistics Provider (3PL). Product deliveries are ad-hoc, and the Route-to-Market (RTM) is not acknowledged as interdependent.
There is awareness of corporate social responsibilities, but policies receive limited buy-in from stakeholders.
Network design encompasses manufacturing operations. An internal logistics plan exists, which can engage a Third-Party Logistics Provider (3PL) to plan and manage distribution for satellite operations. Although the organization aims to develop Route-to-Market (RTM) strategies, there is no understanding of the business impacts. Initial targets have been established for energy reduction.
Central demand buffering is implemented at primary distribution centers (DCs), with limited service-level buffering at satellite DCs. Outsourcing to Third-Party Logistics (3PL) providers is segmented based on their importance to the network.
Product differentiation, configure-to-order, and package-to-order strategies are deployed within the network in close proximity to customers for enhanced efficiency and service.
Real-time visibility into network operations enables a "control tower" approach for efficient monitoring and management. Capital-intensive warehousing logistics capabilities are outsourced to a Fourth-Party Logistics Provider (4PL).
Route-to-Market (RTM) strategies are aligned with the overall business plan and constantly monitored through Integrated Business Planning (IBP). An autonomous way of working facilitates an omnichannel environment for delivering personalized customer experiences.
Operations are managed in isolation, resulting in inconsistent practices and policies across functions. There is a limited understanding of cost and service drivers, leading to low agility in the supply chain to respond to issues effectively.
There is an awareness of how other functions may impact the supply chain. Metrics are defined centrally but are not consistently applied across the organization.
A basic understanding of cost and service drivers in transportation and warehousing exists within the organization.
Supply chain initiatives are coordinated with other functions. Network Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are in place and linked to functional budgets and plans. While there is a good understanding of Cost-to-Serve (CTS) and Customer Service Level (CSL) drivers, control over other aspects of network design remains limited.
Supply chain initiatives are overseen by the head of the supply chain as part of a broader governance structure. Process KPIs differentiate business volume performance from cost profile performance.
The supply chain team is capable of responding to network issues and risks effectively. An established process is continuously reviewed for achieving operational excellence.
Optimized multi-enterprise initiatives achieve joint value realization across collaborating businesses. Network plans are translated into key objectives, KPIs, initiatives, and tasks tailored to each group within the supply chain.
The supply chain team adeptly manages highly volatile network changes with minimal impact on service and cost. A comprehensive IT system strategy has been developed and fully embraced by the organization for enhanced efficiency and performance.

> **Architect Notes**: The model contains all Target Operating Model design layers and a scale that ranges from the least mature level on the left to the highest level of maturity on the right. The letter ‘F’ on the right represents alignment to the future of Supply Chain. In other words, this is where the cutting-edge industry leaders are rated.

It’s important to remember that an organization cannot usually jump more than 2 levels in a given transformation. Progress takes time. Also, achieving a 5 rating in every category is not necessarily advisable. It takes a great amount of focus and resources to get the highest rating. Unless being the industry leader in a given category is key to your company’s strategy, you may want to allocate your time and budget elsewhere.
