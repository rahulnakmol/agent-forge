# Positioning Framework

## Why Positioning Matters

Positioning is not a tagline exercise. It is the strategic act of defining how your product is perceived in the mind of your target customer relative to alternatives. Get it wrong and your best features go unnoticed because customers have no frame of reference for evaluating them.

## April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" Method

The most practical positioning framework comes from April Dunford. It identifies five interdependent components, worked through in a specific order:

### 1. Competitive Alternatives

Start here -- not with your product. What would customers do if your product did not exist? This is rarely a direct competitor. It might be a spreadsheet, a manual process, hiring an intern, or doing nothing at all.

List every realistic alternative, including the status quo. This is the context your product will be evaluated against.

### 2. Unique Attributes

What do you have that the alternatives do not? These must be objectively true and verifiable -- not marketing claims. Examples: "real-time collaboration" (Google Docs vs. emailing Word files), "single binary deployment" (SQLite vs. PostgreSQL).

List every differentiating attribute, even small ones. You will filter later.

### 3. Value (Mapped to Attributes)

For each unique attribute, answer: "So what? What does this enable for the customer?" This is where attributes become value. "Real-time collaboration" becomes "your team never works on a stale version." "Single binary deployment" becomes "no ops team required."

Apply the "so what?" test ruthlessly. If you cannot connect an attribute to a tangible customer outcome, it is not positioning-worthy.

### 4. Target Customer Segment

Who cares the most about the value you just identified? The best positioning targets the segment that values your differentiators the most -- not the largest possible market.

Characteristics of a good target segment:
- They have the problem your value addresses acutely
- They are reachable through identifiable channels
- They buy and adopt quickly (good for word-of-mouth)
- Success with this segment builds credibility for adjacent segments

### 5. Market Category

The market category is the mental shortcut customers use to understand what your product is. Choosing the right category sets expectations, defines your competitive set, and determines which features customers expect as table stakes.

## The Positioning Statement

Combine the five components into a single statement:

> "For [target segment] who [situation/need], [product] is a [market category] that [key value/benefit]. Unlike [competitive alternative], we [primary differentiator]."

### Example

> "For engineering teams who deploy microservices at scale, Meshify is a service mesh platform that eliminates networking complexity. Unlike manual Kubernetes configuration, we provide zero-config service discovery and observability out of the box."

## Category Design: Create vs. Compete

Sometimes the right move is not to compete within an existing category but to create a new one. This is the "Play Bigger" approach (Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead, Kevin Maney).

**Compete in an existing category** when:
- Customers already search for your type of product
- You have a clear, demonstrable advantage over incumbents
- The category is growing and not yet dominated

**Create a new category** when:
- No existing category accurately describes what you do
- Existing categories carry baggage that missets expectations
- You have the resources and patience to educate the market (18-36 months minimum)

Category creation is expensive and slow. Most startups should compete in existing categories with sharp positioning rather than attempting category creation.

## When to Reposition

Reposition when:
- **Market shifts**: a major competitor enters or exits, regulatory changes
- **Product evolution**: your product has grown beyond its original positioning
- **Customer drift**: your best customers are not who you thought they were
- **Stalled growth**: strong product, weak traction often signals a positioning problem

Repositioning is not rebranding. It is re-evaluating the five components in light of new evidence and adjusting the strategic frame. Sometimes the product does not change at all -- only the way you describe it does.

## Common Positioning Mistakes

- **Positioning for everyone**: if your positioning does not exclude anyone, it does not attract anyone
- **Feature-led positioning**: listing features instead of articulating value ("we have 47 integrations" vs. "your tools work together without duct tape")
- **Aspirational positioning**: positioning for the product you wish you had instead of the one you ship today
- **Competitor-obsessed positioning**: defining yourself entirely by what you are not, rather than what you are
