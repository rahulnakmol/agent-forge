# Advanced Techniques: Structural Humanisation & Burstiness Engineering

These techniques go beyond pattern removal to actively engineer the qualities that make writing unmistakably human. Use for Standard and Deep Humanise tiers.

---

## Burstiness Engineering

Burstiness — the natural, uneven rhythm of human writing — is the single most reliable discriminator between human and AI text. AI produces text with remarkably uniform sentence lengths (typically clustering around 18-22 words). Human text varies dramatically.

### Technique 1: The Rhythm Map

Before rewriting, map the desired rhythm for each paragraph:

```
Paragraph 1: SHORT - LONG - MEDIUM - SHORT
Paragraph 2: MEDIUM - MEDIUM - LONG - SHORT - SHORT
Paragraph 3: LONG (single sentence paragraph developing a complex idea)
Paragraph 4: SHORT (two sentences, punchy)
```

Sentence length targets:
- SHORT: 4-10 words
- MEDIUM: 12-22 words
- LONG: 25-45 words

The key: no two consecutive paragraphs should follow the same rhythm pattern.

### Technique 2: Voice-Specific Burstiness

**OX burstiness:** Alternating elegance. A short, direct statement followed by a longer sentence that develops the thought with subordinate clauses and qualifications. The occasional fragment standing alone. The effect is conversational authority — the person who makes a point, then explains why it matters, then moves on.

Example rhythm: "That's the crux of it. [SHORT] The organisations that manage this transition well — and they are fewer than the industry's relentless optimism would suggest — tend to share a quality that has nothing to do with technology budgets or platform choices. [LONG] They listen first. [SHORT]"

**SF burstiness:** High contrast. Very short declarations ("This matters." "Here's why.") alternating with medium-length analytical sentences. Occasional long sentence when building a complex case. The rhythm is decisive — someone who knows what they think and says it.

Example rhythm: "This is broken. [SHORT] Not in the way that you can fix with a patch or a workaround, but fundamentally, structurally broken in a way that means starting the conversation over. [LONG] The good news: starting over is cheaper than you think. [MEDIUM]"

**AB burstiness:** Moderate, steady variation. Mostly medium-length sentences with an occasional short one that lands. Rarely very long — Albertan directness favours getting to the point. The rhythm is unhurried and warm.

Example rhythm: "We spent about six months on this. [MEDIUM] The first three were mostly listening — talking to people on the ground, figuring out what was actually broken versus what the leadership deck said was broken. [LONG] Those are usually different things. [SHORT]"

**ST burstiness:** Architectural contrast. Long, developed sentences building through clauses and subclauses — then a short sentence that distils everything. The rhythm is oratorical: the paragraph builds to its crescendo, then the short sentence lands like a verdict.

Example rhythm: "The paradox of digital transformation — and it is a paradox that the consulting industry has been remarkably slow to confront, despite being handsomely compensated to do precisely that — is that the organisations most in need of change are structurally least capable of absorbing it; their very complexity, the accumulated sediment of decades of incremental decision-making, renders them resistant to the wholesale reimagination that transformation demands. [LONG] The solution, counterintuitively, is to think smaller. [SHORT]"

### Technique 3: The Anchor Sentence

Every paragraph should have one sentence that carries the weight — the sentence the reader remembers. This is the anchor. Place it deliberately:

- At the start (SF style): the declaration that sets up the argument
- At the end (ST style): the conclusion that everything builds toward
- In the middle (OX style): the insight buried in a developing thought
- Varies (AB style): wherever it naturally falls

## Structural Humanisation

### Technique 4: Section Architecture

AI organises predictably: Introduction → Context → Analysis → Conclusion. Break this.

**Options:**
- Start with the most compelling finding, then provide context
- Open with an anecdote or specific case, then zoom out
- Begin with the conclusion, then walk through the reasoning
- Start with what's unknown, then explore what's known
- Open with a contradiction or tension, then resolve it

The key: the structure should feel like it serves the content, not a template.

### Technique 5: Paragraph Asymmetry

Map paragraph lengths before writing:

```
Para 1: 4 sentences (the setup)
Para 2: 1 sentence (the pivot)
Para 3: 6 sentences (the developed argument)
Para 4: 2 sentences (the reaction)
Para 5: 3 sentences (the next point)
```

No two consecutive paragraphs should be the same length. A one-sentence paragraph is powerful. A seven-sentence paragraph is fine if the argument demands it. Uniformity is the enemy.

### Technique 6: Callbacks and Digressions

**Callbacks:** Reference an earlier point later in the text. "Remember the gymnasium membership analogy? It applies here too, but in reverse." This creates a sense of a mind at work — connecting ideas across the piece rather than processing them linearly.

**Digressions:** Allow a brief departure from the main argument when it enriches the text. "This is slightly tangential, but..." or simply weave it in and let the reader experience the author's mind working. The digression must earn its place by adding genuine insight or texture.

### Technique 7: The Imperfect Opening

AI opens with polished, contextualising statements. Humans often open with something more particular:

- A specific observation: "The first thing you notice about the Melbourne office is that nobody uses the standing desks."
- A question: "When did we decide that every strategy needed a transformation?"
- A confession: "I've been thinking about this for weeks and I'm still not sure I've got it right."
- A contradiction: "Everything I'm about to say is undermined by one inconvenient fact."

The opening sets the expectation for the entire piece. If it sounds human, the reader extends that assumption forward.

## Voice-Specific Structural Preferences

### OX Structural Style
- Longer introductory paragraphs that establish context with authority
- Developed middle sections with genuine analytical depth
- Conclusions that are understated rather than grand — ending on a practical observation or a quiet aside rather than a rousing summary
- Comfortable with asides and parenthetical observations that add texture

### SF Structural Style
- Short, punchy openings that state the thesis immediately
- Middle sections organised around evidence and counter-evidence
- Action-oriented conclusions — what to do, not what to think
- Comfortable with numbered steps when they serve clarity (but not as default formatting)

### AB Structural Style
- Openings that establish the practical context: what happened, what was tried, what was learned
- Middle sections that develop through experience and observation rather than abstract analysis
- Conclusions that are practical and forward-looking without being grandiose
- Comfortable with "I don't know" as a structural element — honest uncertainty as a paragraph

### ST Structural Style
- Openings that establish the intellectual frame — the paradox, the historical context, the overlooked dimension
- Middle sections that build architecturally, each paragraph adding a layer to the argument
- Conclusions that feel inevitable — the argument arriving at the only place it could
- Comfortable with the rhetorical question as a structural pivot
- The recommendation paragraph: always present in professional contexts, always clear, always actionable despite the surrounding eloquence

## Anti-Patterns in Structural Humanisation

Do not mistake messiness for humanity. These are failures, not techniques:

- **Random variation** without purpose — changing structure for its own sake
- **Forced informality** — injecting "I mean" and "like" into formal writing
- **Artificial digressions** that don't earn their place
- **Inconsistent voice** — drifting between profiles mid-piece
- **Overcorrection** — making text so deliberately unconventional that it calls attention to its own construction

The goal is writing that feels effortlessly human — not writing that performs its own humanity.
